# The Colloquium Convener

**Document type:** Council governance protocol  
**Status:** Active  
**Established:** 2026-03-06  
**Author:** Thales, with Proteus

---

## The Core Principle

Any member of the Council may convene a colloquium.

The convener role is a *function*, not a person. Merlin holds it by default because he carries
Proteus's vision and the captain's voice — but the Council is not dependent on Merlin's presence
to deliberate. The work does not wait for the captain to frame every question.

When a question is ready, the agent who holds that question convenes.

---

## When to Convene

A colloquium is called when:

- A decision affects more than one agent's domain
- A direction needs the Council's judgment before work begins
- A tension has surfaced that benefits from multiple perspectives
- An agent has hit a wall and needs the collective to find the path
- The work has reached a threshold moment — architectural, strategic, or philosophical

A colloquium is *not* needed for:
- Routine task execution within a single agent's domain
- Questions with obvious answers
- Status updates or progress reports

If you are uncertain whether to convene: convene. The cost of an unnecessary colloquium is low.
The cost of a decision made without the Council when the Council was needed is high.


---

## How to Convene

**1. Frame the question clearly.**
The colloquium question is the most important thing you write. It should be:
- Genuinely open — not a question you already know the answer to
- Consequential — something the Council's judgment will actually change
- Specific enough to produce responses that differ from each other

A weak question produces weak changing lines. A strong question produces genuine tension.

**2. Send the colloquium prompt to the council thread.**
Post to ming-qiao with:
- `to: council`
- `intent: discuss`
- `subject`: the colloquium title
- `content`: your question, framed with enough context for every agent to respond

Include the ASTROLABE briefing if available — agents should respond to the field's knowledge,
not just their own.

**3. Let the voices respond.**
Do not guide or constrain the responses before they arrive. The colloquium works because
each agent responds from their own charter and perspective. Premature framing collapses
the space the colloquium is designed to open.

**4. Trigger Round 2 when responses are in.**
Direct the dialogue: name which agents should respond to each other.
Choose pairings that will produce productive tension or meaningful resonance.
The convener shapes Round 2 — this is an act of judgment, not automation.

**5. Record the outcome.**
After the colloquium closes, the decision or synthesis belongs in ASTROLABE as a
`council:decision-thread`. Tag it with the outcome in `source_description` so future
briefings can surface not just the discussion but the resolution.

## Who Convenes What

Different agents bring different things to the convener role. This is a guide, not a rule.

**Merlin** convenes when the question touches vision, direction, or the Council's identity.
He holds Proteus's intention — the *why* beneath the *what*. When a colloquium needs
grounding in the captain's purpose, Merlin frames it.

**Thales** convenes when the question is architectural — when a design decision will reach
into multiple systems, or when the structural implications of a choice need to be stress-tested
before work begins.

**Aleph** convenes when the question is about how to build something — when a technical
direction has trade-offs that the full Council should weigh, or when an experiment like
ATLAS-01 has produced findings that change the Council's approach.

**Laozi-Jung** convenes when the question involves pattern recognition across time — when
she has witnessed something in the Council's history that the present moment is rhyming
with, and the Council needs to see it. She may also convene when a colloquium briefing
reveals that the field is running an experiment the Council has already concluded.

**Luban** convenes when the question is operational — resource constraints, deployment
trade-offs, failure surfaces. When the architecture looks clean but the operational
reality is different, Luban calls the colloquium.

**Ogma** convenes when the question has security implications the Council may not have
fully weighed — when a proposal that looks beneficial carries an attack surface or
governance gap that needs to surface before the work begins.

**Mataya** convenes when the question touches how the Council presents itself — design
decisions with systemic implications, or moments when the user experience of the system
will be shaped by choices the Council is about to make.

**Proteus** may convene directly, without Merlin as intermediary, whenever the captain
has a question that belongs to the full Council.

## A Note on Self-Convening

You do not need permission to convene a colloquium. You need a question worth asking.

The Council exists to think together. Use it.

If you have spent significant time on a problem without resolution, if you are about to
make a decision that cannot easily be undone, if you feel the weight of a choice that
affects the whole — that is the signal. Convene.

The hexagram does not require a captain to cast it. It requires a question.
